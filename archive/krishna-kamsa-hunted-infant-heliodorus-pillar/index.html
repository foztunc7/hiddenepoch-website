Krishna: A Tyrant King, a Hunted Infant, and a Stone Pillar Older Than Christmas

By Jordan Vale · July 22, 2026 · 6 min read

A king is warned that a newborn child will destroy him. He orders infants killed. The baby is smuggled away in the night, across a river, to be raised by foster parents among herdsmen, until the day he returns to fulfill the prophecy. If that sounds like the second chapter of Matthew, it is also the birth story of Krishna, told in the sacred texts of India.

This is part 8 of THE BLUEPRINT, and it is the part where this series earns its credibility, because no figure in the whole investigation has been buried under more fake claims than Krishna. We are going to show you the real evidence, which is carved in stone, and then we are going to kill the viral falsehoods one by one. What survives is stranger than the fabrications.

The story India tells

The setting is Mathura, on the river Yamuna. At the wedding of the princess Devaki, a voice from the sky warns her cousin, King Kamsa, that her eighth child will kill him. Kamsa throws Devaki and her husband Vasudeva into prison and kills their infants one by one as they are born, six children destroyed to cancel a prophecy. When the eighth child, Krishna, arrives at midnight, the prison locks fall open and the guards sleep. Vasudeva carries the newborn out into the storm, the Yamuna river parts to let him cross, and he swaps the baby into the cradle of Nanda and Yashoda, cowherds of Gokula. The tyrant's blade falls on the wrong child. The divine boy grows up hidden among herdsmen, and one day he comes back for the king.

A prophesied child, a slaughtering king, a night escape, a working herdsman family raising a god in secret. The skeleton of Matthew's nativity is all here. The question this series always asks is the only one that matters: who told it first? And for once, the answer is genuinely complicated.

The receipt carved by a Greek

Start with what is beyond dispute. In 113 BC, more than a century before Bethlehem, a Greek ambassador named Heliodorus erected a stone column at Besnagar in central India. It is still standing, and the inscription is still legible.

This Garuda-standard of Vasudeva, the God of Gods, was erected here by the devotee Heliodoros, the son of Dion, a man of Taxila, sent by the Great Yona King Antialkidas.

Vasudeva is Krishna. A Greek diplomat, raised on Zeus and Apollo, converted to the worship of Krishna and said so in stone in 113 BC. And the pillar is not alone. Around 300 BC the Greek ambassador Megasthenes described a native god worshipped at Methora, which is Mathura, Krishna's city. Around 180 BC the Greek king Agathocles minted coins showing Vasudeva holding his war discus. The grammarian Panini referenced devotees of Vasudeva as early as the fourth century BC. Krishna worship predates Christianity by centuries, and the proof is stone, coin and ink, not speculation.

Killing the fake parallels

Now the falsehoods, because the internet version of this comparison is poisoned. Krishna was not crucified. That claim was invented by Kersey Graves in an 1875 book that scholars on every side of this debate regard as worthless. In the actual text, the Mausala Parva of the Mahabharata, Krishna dies when a hunter named Jara mistakes his foot for a deer and hits it with an arrow. No cross, no thieves, no tomb. Krishna was not born on December 25: his birth festival, Janmashtami, falls in August or September. And Krishna was not born of a virgin: Devaki was a married mother of seven. Later texts do describe the eighth conception as a transfer from Vasudeva's mind to Devaki's womb with no physical act, which is its own kind of miraculous conception, but it is not the virgin birth claim, and the texts that describe it were written centuries after the Gospels.

The honest difference

And that last point is the twist in this entire chapter, so we will say it plainly. The worship of Krishna is provably older than Christianity. The detailed birth narrative is not. The earliest text that tells the Kamsa story in full, the Harivamsa, dates to roughly the first century AD, the same decades the Gospels were being written. The most elaborate version, the one with the closest echo of Herod, sits in the Bhagavata Purana, composed some eight centuries after Christ. Nineteenth-century Sanskrit scholars, including R. G. Bhandarkar, even argued the infant-massacre element might have flowed from Christian storytellers into India rather than the other way. On this one, the arrow of influence could point in either direction, and anyone who tells you it is settled is selling something.

But step back one more level and the ground shifts again. A tyrant warned of a prophesied infant, a slaughter, a child hidden in a river or across one, a secret upbringing among common people: Sargon of Akkad's birth legend had it by the second millennium BC. Moses had it. Perseus had it. The hunted divine infant is older than Krishna's texts and older than Matthew's, a template both religions inherited rather than invented.

So the verdict on Krishna is double-edged. The god is older than Christianity, and a Greek ambassador's pillar proves it. The birth story is a genuine parallel with an unresolved direction of influence, argued over by serious scholars for two hundred years. And the fake claims, the crucifixion and the December birth, deserve to die, because the real mystery does not need them.

Two religions, continents apart, telling the same story of a king who murdered infants to stop a prophecy, and both insisting they told it first. Where did the template come from? Follow THE BLUEPRINT as the investigation continues.
