The Books On Our Shelf

If you want to actually understand the civilizations Hidden Epoch investigates — Sumer, Babylon, the Hittites, the wider Ancient Near East — these four are where to start. Primary scholarship, not pop history. We use them as source material.

Survey · Ancient Near East

The Ancient Near East: A History

William W. Hallo & William Kelly Simpson●1997 · Cengage

The single best one-volume textbook for getting your bearings across the entire region — Sumer, Akkad, Babylon, Assyria, Egypt, the Hittites, Persia. Used in serious undergraduate Near Eastern programs for two decades. Read this first if you want a frame to hang the rest on.

Get on Amazon → Sumer

The Sumerians: Their History, Culture, and Character

Samuel Noah Kramer●1971 · University of Chicago

Kramer is the man who taught the world to read Sumerian. He translated the original tablets the rest of the field is still arguing over — including the flood story that predates Genesis by 1,500 years. The chapter on Sumerian religion alone is worth the price.

Get on Amazon → Babylon · Mesopotamia

Babylon: Mesopotamia and the Birth of Civilization

Paul Kriwaczek●2012 · St. Martin's Griffin

Three thousand years of Mesopotamia in one readable volume. Kriwaczek argues that almost everything we call "civilization" — cities, writing, law, banking, astronomy, the seven-day week — was invented between the Tigris and the Euphrates and exported from there. The case is hard to argue with by the time he's done.

Get on Amazon → Hittites

The Kingdom of the Hittites

Trevor Bryce●2006 · Oxford University Press

The definitive academic history of the empire that was lost for three thousand years and only confirmed in the early 1900s — even though the Bible had been naming the Hittites the whole time. Bryce reconstructs the politics, the treaties, the religion, and the collapse from the cuneiform archives at Hattusa.
